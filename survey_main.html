Policy Survey

Health at a Glance: Latin America and the Caribbean

Thematic chapter on quality-of-care policies

This questionnaire is designed to collect information from LAC countries on quality-of-care policies and overall government efforts to assure and improve the quality of healthcare. The work based on this information will be released in 2026 as a thematic chapter of the third edition of Health at a Glance: Latin America and the Caribbean, co-published by the OECD, the World Bank and the Inter-American Development Bank.

The questionnaire has two parts. Part I maps the state of play in quality-of-care policies—what exists in law and governance, the standards that guide practice, how performance is measured and improved, and how patients and professionals are engaged. Part II asks about the digital health capabilities and the uses of health data for quality improvement.

Definition of key terms used in this questionnaire

  • Hospital: Establishments primarily engaged in providing medical, diagnostic, and treatment services that include physician, nursing, and other health services to in-patients and the specialised accommodation services required by in-patients. Hospitals may also provide out-patient services as a secondary activity.
  • Primary Health Care (PHC): Primary care is the first level of contact of individuals, the family and community with the (national) health system bringing health care as close as possible to where people live and work and provides continuing and comprehensive medical care to individuals and families in an ambulatory setting. It may be provided by general practitioners (or "family doctors") and their teams but could also be provided by out-patient departments of first level hospitals.
  • Health care quality: Healthcare quality comprises three core dimensions of care—effectiveness, safety and patient-centredness (responsiveness). These dimensions are applied across the care pathway: preventive, acute, chronic, and palliative care.

1.4 Accreditation and other external quality assessment mechanisms

Accreditation and other external quality assessment mechanisms provide structured, independent verification that health care organizations meet defined standards and continuously improve. Originating as profession-led initiatives, accreditation programs have evolved with growing government involvement while generally remaining accountable to autonomous governing bodies. They apply broadly consistent, health care–specific standards and emphasize bottom-up improvement through self-assessment, peer review, and management reporting, with recognition granted to organizations that meet requirements; these programs now extend from hospitals to primary care. Complementing accreditation, certification evaluates conformity with international standards such as ISO 9001 through audits by recognized, independent bodies operating under ISO 19011, producing findings on compliance and noncompliance without prescribing corrective recommendations.

In the European system, an inspectorate focuses on ensuring a health facility's compliance with mandatory legal and regulatory standards, acting as a government-mandated oversight body to enforce minimum requirements, while accreditation is an independent, external assessment of a facility against a set of voluntary, pre-defined quality standards by a recognized body, providing public recognition of quality and facilitating mutual recognition of services across borders. The key difference lies in their purpose: inspection ensures legal compliance and minimum safety, whereas accreditation demonstrates a higher level of quality and commitment to excellence beyond basic legal requirements. 1.7  Audit and performance review

There are several ways to assess quality of care in a health system. Below is a short description of the main approaches:

  • Audit studies on particular topics where quality problems are expected. These targeted clinical reviews (e.g., perinatal mortality, post-operative outcomes, anaesthesia adverse events) use records review to identify preventable harm and prompt corrective action.
  • National quality indicator sets covering structure, process, and outcomes. These standardised measures are applied across hospitals, outpatient/primary care and long-term care to track performance and drive improvement.
  • Public reporting of provider performance. Public dashboards or reports present comparable provider or system results to support transparency, accountability, and informed choice.
  • Regular national quality/performance report. Many countries publish periodic (e.g., annual) syntheses that combine audit findings and indicator results, highlight trends and disparities, and recommend actions.
